6. Integration of Multiple Perspectives

aka: integral approach

Key Ideas:

We humans often take into account multiple perspectives on a thing or situation, do our best to integrate these different views into some coherent mental model, and then decide upon some action based upon a balanced consideration. We can think of this as taking an integral approach.

We humans have the ability to see things – people, events, circumstances – from more than one perspective.

More than that, things appear differently when we view them from different perspectives.

In other words, you and I may observe the same event, or consider the same issue, and yet see that same event or issue very differently.

Not only that, but a single person may view an event or issue from multiple perspectives.

Here’s an example of what I mean that I came across recently in the news. It’s an excerpt from an interview with former US President Barack Obama, speaking about immigration into America.

Immigration is tough. It always has been because, on the one hand, I think we are naturally a people that wants to help others. And we see tragedy and hardship and families that are desperately trying to get here so that their kids are safe, and they’re in some cases fleeing violence or catastrophe. At the same time, we’re a nation state. We have borders. The idea that we can just have open borders is something that … as a practical matter, is unsustainable.

If we consider Obama’s words for a moment, we can easily see that he’s describing two different perspectives on a single issue.

  • First there is what we might call an empathic perspective: we see people who are hurting and we want to help them, or want them to be helped;
  • Second there is what we might call a systemic perspective, describing our current system of world and national governance, with nation-states and borders and legal systems.

But this way of considering issues, through consideration of multiple perspectives, is not unique to Obama, or to the topic of immigration: it’s just part and parcel of how we humans have learned to deal with the world around us.

So what do we do with these varied points of view?

It’s often tempting – and certainly simpler – to bless one view as the “right” one, and ignore or reject all others as false.

It’s more challenging – but sometimes more useful, and at times more equitable – to try to integrate these varied perspectives into a more comprehensive understanding of a person, event or issue.

When we try to integrate varied perspectives in this way, we can think of this broadly as taking an integral approach.
